This project was an exploration into Reinforcement Learning (RL) and reward shaping within the CARLA autonomous driving simulator. The goal was to train and compare two distinct Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) agents: one "Anxious" and one "Confident."

### The Experiment
The project focused on how different reward structures influence driving behavior. We compared two primary functions:

**Standard Reward ($$R_1$$):** Focused on simple lane keeping and speed maintenance.
$$R_1(s_t) = r_{cross} + r_{speed} + r_{collision}$$

**Anxious Reward ($$R_2$$):** Included a much higher penalty for risk and lane deviation.
$$R_2(s_t) = r_{risk} + r_{collision} + r_{waypoint\_progress} + r_{target\_progress}$$

### Key Lessons Learned
* **The "Conservative" Trap:** While the Anxious reward structure successfully encouraged safer behavior, it often made the agent too conservative to navigate effectively. 
* **Reward Engineering:** Simply increasing the "anxiety" of the agent (via higher penalties for drifting) wasn't a silver bullet; the experiment highlighted the necessity for more nuanced reward design or hybrid algorithms to achieve balanced driving.
* **Resource Management:** I practiced tuning and training models within the constraints of limited GPU resources, learning how to optimize training runs for efficiency.

This was a fun project that provided hands-on experience with the complexities of autonomous systems and the delicate balance of reward functions in RL.
